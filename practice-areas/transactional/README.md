# Transactional Practice

**Focus:** Creating and reviewing legal documents that bring parties together—deals, not disputes.

---

## Overview

Transactional attorneys draft and negotiate contracts, conduct due diligence, advise on compliance, and manage deal timelines. Work is **collaborative** and **forward-looking**; the goal is to complete a deal and prevent future conflict. They rarely appear in court.

## How Transactional Attorneys Operate

### Workflow

- **Deal intake** → Due diligence → Document drafting/negotiation → Closing checklist → Closing
- **Steadier pace** with intensity spikes near closing dates
- **Multi-party coordination** (buyers, sellers, lenders, agents, title companies)
- **Document-heavy**—checklists, exhibits, signature pages, closing books

### Key Skills

- Preventive thinking and business acumen
- Strong drafting and negotiation
- Attention to detail
- Deadline and checklist discipline

### Things to Keep in Mind

1. **Checklists are non-negotiable**—especially for real estate and corporate closings. Missing one item can kill a deal or create liability.
2. **"Time is of the essence"**—contract provisions can make deadlines firm and non-negotiable.
3. **Document version control**—multiple parties, multiple drafts. Use clear naming and track changes.
4. **Trust accounting**—escrow, deposits, and closing funds require strict compliance.
5. **Long-term client relationships**—transactional work often leads to repeat engagements; relationship management matters.

## Pain Points (Reddit, Industry)

- **Document chaos**—endless email threads, missing documents, forgotten follow-ups
- **No standardized CRM**—real estate attorneys lack central dashboards for deadlines, documents, deposits
- **Checklist discipline**—commercial closings without checklists risk overlooking critical items
- **Tool fragmentation**—managing Clio + Dropbox + DocuSign + spreadsheets creates workflow gaps
- **Scaling**—midsize firms find legacy systems (Tabs, Practice Master) insufficient as they grow
