# Associates

**Category:** Attorneys and Legal Practitioners  
**Approval Level:** Medium—work product requires partner review before client delivery

---

## Role Summary

Associates are employed attorneys who represent the firm, handle daily substantive case tasks, and work under partner supervision. They are typically on a partnership track and are developed through increasingly complex work and client exposure.

---

## Industry Job Description

*Sourced from BetterTeam, LegalClarity, MB Attorney Search, CLOC, and law firm postings.*

### Formal Summary

An Associate Attorney is a licensed legal professional employed by a law firm, typically early in their career, working under partner supervision. They serve as the backbone of law firms, handling much of the daily legal workload while working toward potential partnership. Core duties include: legal research (analyzing laws, regulations, and case precedent), document drafting (motions, pleadings, contracts, briefs), client communication (meeting with clients, providing updates), court preparation (organizing evidence, discovery responses, witness questions), case support (depositions, hearings, trials), client representation (hearings, negotiations, meetings), administrative tasks (deadlines, filings, confidential records), and business development (networking, legal publications).

### Typical Qualifications

- **Education:** Juris Doctor (J.D.) from accredited law school; passing score on state bar examination
- **Experience:** 0–6 years; responsibilities vary by firm size and practice area
- **Skills:** Legal research, drafting, discovery, court preparation, time management, matter organization

### Key Distinctions by Firm Size

- **Large firms:** Support complex cases as part of a team; may specialize in specific legal areas
- **Smaller firms:** May manage own client matters with lighter supervision

## Detailed Responsibilities

### 1. Legal Research and Analysis

- **Research memos:** Conduct thorough legal research; prepare clear, well-cited memos with conclusions and recommendations.
- **Case law and statutory analysis:** Identify relevant authority; distinguish adverse authority; summarize holdings and applicability.
- **Cite checking:** Verify citations for accuracy and proper format (Bluebook, local rules).
- **Research tools:** Use Westlaw, Lexis, and other research platforms efficiently; document search strategies for reproducibility.

### 2. Drafting and Document Preparation

- **Pleadings and motions:** Draft complaints, answers, motions, briefs, and supporting documents under partner direction.
- **Discovery:** Draft interrogatories, requests for production, requests for admission; prepare responses and objections.
- **Correspondence:** Draft client letters, demand letters, and internal memoranda. Ensure tone and content are appropriate.
- **Agreements:** Draft and revise contracts, releases, and other transactional documents as assigned.

### 3. Discovery and Case Development

- **Document review:** Participate in document review; identify key documents; prepare privilege logs and production logs.
- **Deposition preparation:** Assist with deposition outlines, exhibit preparation, and witness preparation.
- **Trial preparation:** Assist with trial exhibits, witness lists, jury instructions, and trial binders.
- **E-discovery:** Coordinate with [Document & Litigation Support Specialist](../document-litigation-support-specialist/README.md) on productions and filings.

### 4. Court and Client Support

- **Court appearances:** Attend hearings, conferences, and depositions as assigned. Prepare thoroughly and report back.
- **Client communications:** Communicate with clients under partner supervision; document all communications.
- **Calendar management:** Track deadlines, court dates, and internal milestones. Escalate conflicts or capacity issues.
- **Conflict checks:** Run conflict checks before new matters; document results.

### 5. Time and Matter Management

- **Time entry:** Enter time contemporaneously; use clear, matter-specific narratives. Meet firm billing expectations.
- **Matter organization:** Maintain organized matter files; use firm systems (Clio, etc.) for tasks, documents, and deadlines.
- **Capacity management:** Communicate workload to assigning partners; flag overcommitment early.
- **Professional development:** Complete CLE; seek feedback; participate in firm training and mentoring.
